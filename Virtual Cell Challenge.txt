class="Evaluation_sectionContent__GRdzp"><p>Predictive models can be trained to generalize along several axes. Two core dimensions are (i) generalization across biological context (e.g. cell type, cell line, culture conditions, or even in vivo versus in vitro settings) and (ii) generalization to novel genetic and/or chemical perturbations, including their combinations.<br><br>This challenge focuses on context generalization as a highly challenging real-world task: <span class="font-[510]">participants will predict the effects of single-gene perturbations in a test cell type (H1)</span>. The transcriptomic effect of these genetic perturbations has been previously reported in at least one other cellular context. This reflects a common experimental reality: testing all perturbations in every context is impractical due to cost, yet accurate context-specific predictions are crucial because responses depend on factors like cell type, state, differentiation stage, culture conditions, and genetic background.</p><div class="flex flex-col items-center p-6 "><img alt="Challenge Task Figure" loading="lazy" width="987" height="525" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" class="hidden md:inline-block" src="./Virtual Cell Challenge_files/challenge-task.a3fb3c19.svg" style="color: transparent;"><img alt="Challenge Task Figure" loading="lazy" width="559" height="1036" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" class="inline-block md:hidden" src="./Virtual Cell Challenge_files/challenge-task-vertical.05122527.svg" style="color: transparent;"></div><p>Given that most published single cell genetic perturbation datasets span only a handful of cell lines, true zero-shot generalization to new cell states is likely premature. A more appropriate strategy at this stage is few-shot adaptation, where a subset of perturbations in the new cellular context is provided to guide model generalization. To support this, we provide expression profiles for a subset of perturbations measured directly in H1 hESCs, enabling participants to adapt their models before predicting responses to the remaining unseen perturbations in the same cell type.</p></div></div><div class="Evaluation_section__etn9p" id="submissions"><div class="Evaluation_sectionTitle__Lc3P1"><div class="Evaluation_sectionTitleIcon__2UEyj"><img alt="Submissions Icon" loading="lazy" width="36" height="36" decoding="async" data-nimg="1" src="./Virtual Cell Challenge_files/submissions.b4413beb.svg" style="color: transparent;"></div><h4>Submissions</h4></div><div class="Evaluation_sectionContent__GRdzp"><p>Competitors will submit prediction files in .vcc file format, generated via the the <a href="https://pypi.org/project/cell-eval/" class="Evaluation_textLink__1qQlb" target="_blank">cell-eval</a> Python module. During the initial phase of the Challenge, competitors' results will show on the live rankings public leaderboard. In the final week of the Challenge, a "final test set" will be released. During the final week, competitors will submit final results, which will not appear on the leaderboard. The winners of the Challenge will be announced in December based on final submissions.<br><br>The results file must contain gene expression counts caused by each identified perturbation in the validation dataset (during the live ranking period) and final test dataset (during the final week of the Challenge), as predicted by competitors' models.</p><div class="flex flex-col gap-2 bg-gray-12 p-8 rounded-2xl"><h5>Submission File Requirements</h5><p>For a full walkthrough of generating a valid prediction file, see our tutorial in <a href="https://github.com/ArcInstitute/cell-eval/blob/main/tutorials/vcc/vcc.ipynb" class="Evaluation_textLink__1qQlb" target="_blank">cell-eval</a>.
